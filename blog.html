Redefining Solar Durability: The Rise of Polyurethane Composites

For decades, aluminum has been the default material for solar panel framing — cheap, abundant, and easy to manufacture. But aluminum comes with a hidden cost: it corrodes, expands in heat, and fails prematurely in coastal and desert environments where solar demand is highest.

Curion's pultruded polyurethane composite profiles are changing that equation. With a thermal expansion coefficient 60% lower than aluminum and zero susceptibility to galvanic corrosion, composite frames are now outlasting aluminum structures by 10–15 years in accelerated aging tests.

The Carbon Footprint of Solar Framing: Why the Material You Choose Matters as Much as the Panel

The photovoltaic industry has spent two decades perfecting cell efficiency while almost entirely ignoring one critical factor: the embodied carbon of the structural components that hold those cells in place.

A standard aluminum extrusion frame contributes 42 kg CO₂-equivalent per panel across its lifetime — accounting for extraction, smelting, fabrication, and end-of-life disposal. Curion's glass-fibre reinforced polyurethane composite frames reduce that number by up to 85%, to just 6.3 kg CO₂-equivalent.
